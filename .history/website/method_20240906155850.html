Method Details

Normalization to the maximum voluntary contraction (MVC) in the same task/context as the task of interest (with matched contraction type*, muscle length/joint angle, and/or velocity) *isometric; concentric; eccentric

Yes: High probability that this is appropriate

Normalizing to maximum voluntary contraction (MVC) provides a relative value useful to compare the magnitude of muscle activity in different tasks or conditions. This represents the activity as a proportion of the maximum capacity of the muscle, as long as the normalization contraction is collected in the same position, velocity, and contraction types as the task of interest.

Definition of the method

  • MVC provides a meaningful reference â€“ results can be interpreted with respect to the maximum capacity of that muscle in that context.
  • The amplitude of EMG signals has the potential to provide an estimate of the changes in muscle force during a contraction where the length and velocity of the muscle fibres are minimally changed (isometric) and in the absence of fatigue. Because the value is a proportion of MVC, it will increase almost linearly with force, and it might be possible to infer the force change per unit change in %MVC.
  • Because the investigated task and the MVC task are the same, it is more likely that the EMG amplitude represents the true maximum muscle activation that is possible in that context.

  • If the tasks or conditions to be compared are different (e.g., changes in contraction type, muscle length/joint angle, and/or velocity), each task or condition needs to be normalized separately in a task/condition-specific manner, because the amplitude recorded during the MVC might be different.
  • For this reason, normalizing to MVC during dynamic conditions can be challenging and sometimes shortcuts are taken, for example, this might involve performing MVC in a subset of contexts (but not all). In that case, it is important to identify this as a potential limitation.
  • Some participants are potentially unable or not willing to perform a true MVC. For instance, it might not be an appropriate method for participants with recent surgery or participants with pain.
  • Participants and Investigators might not know if they have performed a true maximum (some methods can determine this but are not possible/feasible for all muscles).
  • Finally, maximum performance of the task might not induce the maximum possible activation of the target muscle. This is particularly relevant in dynamic conditions, or when the task during MVC involves multiple muscles that might not contribute equally to the performance of the task.

  • Conduct between 3-5 MVC that generate consistent values.
  • Include adequate rest periods (1-2 minutes) that will depend on the duration of the maximal efforts.
  • The task executed during MVC must resemble as closely as possible to the task of interest but performed to the maximal capacity of the muscle investigated.
  • Try to ensure that the participant performs a true maximum (e.g., encourage them strongly, consider twitch interpolation [hyperlink with explanation of concept] when possible).
